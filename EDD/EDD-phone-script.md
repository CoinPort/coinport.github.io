# EDD Phone Script — Scam Risk Check for Vulnerable Customers

**Purpose:** This script is used by CoinPort staff conducting Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) phone calls with customers identified as high risk of being a scam victim — including customers over 60 years old, customers in rural or regional areas, and customers whose transaction pattern otherwise triggered EDD review under the AML/CTF Program.

**Goal of the call:** Determine, through open and non-judgemental conversation, whether the customer understands who they are sending funds to and why — and identify any signs of scam coercion before funds are released.

**Do not** read this script word-for-word like a form. Use it as a guide and let the conversation flow naturally. Vulnerable customers, especially those being coached by a scammer, respond better to a warm, unhurried conversation than to a rapid checklist.

---

## Before you call

- Review the customer's account: transaction history, destination address/exchange (if known), amount, and what triggered the EDD flag.
- Note the customer's age bracket and address (rural/regional vs metro) so you can tailor pacing and tone, but never mention age or location as the reason for the call.
- Have the [EDD email](EDD-email.md) content in mind — the scam categories and questions there are the basis for this script.
- Block out enough time. Do not rush a vulnerable customer off the phone.

## Call framing

Never say "we think you might be a scam victim" or "you're high risk" as an opener. Frame the call as a routine, standard check that CoinPort does for all customers before larger or first-time transactions. This avoids embarrassing the customer and avoids alerting a scammer who may be coaching them.

---

## 1. Opening

> "Hi, is this {{FirstName}}? My name is {{StaffName}}, I'm calling from CoinPort. Is now an okay time for a quick chat? It'll only take a few minutes."

If it's not a good time, offer to call back. Do not proceed with sensitive questions if the customer sounds rushed, distracted, or like someone else is prompting them to end the call.

> "I'm calling because as part of our standard account checks, we give customers a call before processing [this transaction / your first transaction] — it's something we do for everyone, just to make sure everything's in order and to check in on how you're finding CoinPort so far."

## 2. Identity verification

Confirm you are speaking to the account holder, not a third party.

- "Can you confirm your full name and date of birth for me, please?"
- "And just to check, is this the [phone number / email] linked to your account?"

**Red flag:** Someone other than the account holder is on the call, answering on their behalf, or feeding them answers in the background.

## 3. Open the conversation — let them explain in their own words

Start broad. Let the customer tell their own story before asking pointed questions — this is where inconsistencies or scam scripting show up most clearly.

- "Can you tell me a bit about what you're using CoinPort for at the moment?"
- "And this transaction we're calling about — can you walk me through what that's for?"

Listen for: hesitation, vague answers, reciting language that sounds rehearsed or copied ("investment opportunity," "trading platform," "recovery agent"), or reluctance to explain.

## 4. Targeted questions

Work through whichever of these are relevant to what the customer has described. Keep it conversational — pick up on what they say rather than reading down the list mechanically.

**Who they're sending to**
- "Have you met this person face-to-face?"
- "How did you first get in contact with them?" (dating app, social media, phone call, online ad, message from a stranger)
- "How long have you known them?"

**The opportunity or relationship**
- "Has anyone promised you a guaranteed return, or told you this is a limited-time opportunity?"
- "Are they helping you trade or invest through a website, app, or dashboard they showed you?"
- "Has anyone asked you to install software like AnyDesk, TeamViewer, or AirDroid, or to share your screen with them?"
- "Have they asked you to keep this money transfer private — from family, from your bank, or from us?"

**Prior losses / recovery scams**
- "Have you sent money or crypto to anyone before as part of this? Has anyone contacted you offering to help recover money you've previously lost?"
- "Has anyone claiming to be a lawyer, police officer, government official, or 'hacker' asked you for an upfront fee to release funds?"

**Impersonation**
- "Did anyone contact you claiming to be from your bank, the ATO, myGov, police, or even CoinPort, saying your money or identity was at risk?"

**Money mule risk**
- "Has anyone asked you to receive crypto or money and forward it on to someone else? Has anyone asked to use your CoinPort account, or asked you to share your login details?"

**Pressure and secrecy**
- "Do you feel any pressure or urgency to send this today?"
- "Has anyone told you what to say if we, or your bank, asked about this payment?"

## 5. Vulnerability-sensitive checks (use judgement — don't make it feel like an interrogation)

- Check the customer is comfortable and not being watched or coached: "Is it just you there at the moment?"
- If something feels off but the customer is guarded, gently suggest involving someone they trust: "Would you like to talk this over with a family member or friend before going ahead? There's no rush on our end."
- For customers who seem confused about the technical side (wallets, exchanges, "trading bots"), slow down and check understanding rather than assuming comprehension: "Just so I've explained that clearly — can you tell me in your own words where this money is going?"

## 6. Closing the call

### If no red flags identified
> "Thanks so much for talking that through with me, {{FirstName}}. Everything sounds in order, so we'll go ahead and process this for you. If anything changes, or if you're ever unsure about a payment, you can always call us before sending — we'd much rather double-check than have you lose money."

### If red flags identified, or you're not fully satisfied
Do **not** accuse the customer of being scammed. Do not process the transaction yet.

> "Thanks for talking me through that. Based on what you've described, I'd like to pause this transaction for now while we look into it a bit further — this is just a precaution we take to protect our customers, and it doesn't cost you anything. Someone from our team will follow up with you shortly."

Then:
- Hold/flag the transaction pending compliance review.
- Escalate immediately to the AML/CTF Compliance Officer (Nicanor Nuqui) with full call notes.
- Consider whether a Suspicious Matter Report (SMR) obligation is triggered.
- Send the customer the [EDD scam-awareness email](EDD-email.md) as a follow-up if not already sent.

## 7. Record keeping

Document, in the customer's file, in line with AML/CTF record-keeping obligations:

- Date/time of call and staff member who made it.
- Summary of the customer's explanation of the transaction.
- Any red flags identified (or confirmation that none were).
- Outcome: transaction approved, held for review, or escalated.
- If escalated, who it was escalated to and any follow-up action taken.

---

## Quick reference — red flags to listen for

- Never met the recipient in person; relationship started online or via an unsolicited message/call.
- Promises of guaranteed, high, or risk-free returns.
- A "trading dashboard," "bot," or platform shown to them by the other party.
- Remote access or screen-sharing software requested.
- Told to keep the transaction secret, or told what to say if asked about it.
- Upfront fee requested to release, unlock, or recover funds.
- Contact initiated by someone claiming to be a bank, government agency, police, or CoinPort itself.
- Asked to receive and forward funds, or to share account login details.
- Pressure, urgency, or a "limited time" framing.
- Customer seems coached, hesitant, or unable to explain the transaction in their own words.
- Someone else appears to be present, listening, or answering on the customer's behalf.
