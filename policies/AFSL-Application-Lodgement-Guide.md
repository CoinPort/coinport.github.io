### **1.0 PURPOSE**

This document provides a practical, step-by-step guide to lodging an Australian Financial Services Licence (AFSL) application with the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC). It is a companion document to the AFSL Application Project Plan (`policies/AFSL-Application-Project-Plan.md`, CP-COMP-007) and is intended to be used during **Phase 5: Application Preparation and Lodgement**.

---

### **2.0 ASIC AFSL APPLICATION OVERVIEW**

AFSL applications are lodged electronically through the **ASIC Connect** portal. The application form is designated **FS01 — Application for AFS Licence**. ASIC assesses applications against the general obligations in **section 912A of the Corporations Act 2001** and the requirements set out in the relevant Regulatory Guides (RG 1, RG 2, RG 104, RG 105, RG 166).

### **4.0 STEP-BY-STEP LODGEMENT PROCESS**

---

#### **Step 1: Register for ASIC Connect (if not already registered)**

- Navigate to **https://connectonline.asic.gov.au**
- If CoinPort does not already have an ASIC Connect account, register for one using the company's ACN (624 879 223)
- ASIC Connect uses **myGovID** for identity verification — the lodging officer (Compliance Officer or Director) must have a myGovID linked to the company via **Relationship Authorisation Manager (RAM)**
- **RAM Portal:** https://info.authorisationmanager.gov.au
- Ensure at least two authorised persons have access to ASIC Connect for business continuity

**Action Items:**
- [ ] Confirm ASIC Connect access for Compliance Officer
- [ ] Confirm ASIC Connect access for at least one Director
- [ ] Verify myGovID and RAM authorisations are current

---

#### **Step 2: Review the ASIC AFS Licensing Kit**

Before commencing the application form, thoroughly review:

1. **RG 1** — Provides the overall framework, including a flowchart of the application process and a checklist of what ASIC expects
2. **RG 2** — The primary preparation guide; explains each section of the FS01 form and what supporting documents are required
3. **ASIC's online guidance** at https://asic.gov.au/for-finance-professionals/afs-licensees/applying-for-and-managing-an-afs-licence/applying-for-an-afs-licence/

**Tip:** RG 2 contains an **Application Checklist** (Appendix) that maps every question in the FS01 form to the required evidence. Use this as a master tracking document.

---

#### **Step 3: Complete the FS01 Application Form**

The FS01 form is completed online through ASIC Connect. It can be **saved as a draft** and returned to before final submission. The form has the following major sections:

| Section | Content Required |
|---|---|
| **Part A — Applicant Details** | Legal name, ACN/ABN, registered office, principal place of business, contact details |
| **Part B — Business Description** | Nature and scope of financial services to be provided; types of clients (retail, wholesale, or both); how services will be delivered |
| **Part C — Authorisations Sought** | Specific financial services and financial products to be authorised (see Section 4.1 of the Project Plan) |
| **Part D — Responsible Managers** | Full details for each RM: name, qualifications, experience, competency pathway (RG 105), statutory declaration |
| **Part E — Key Personnel** | Directors, secretaries, controllers, and other key persons; fit and proper declarations |
| **Part F — Organisational Competence** | How the applicant will maintain organisational competence; training arrangements; outsourcing arrangements |
| **Part G — Compliance Arrangements** | Summary of compliance plan, monitoring, breach reporting, and dispute resolution |
| **Part H — Risk Management** | Summary of risk management framework |
| **Part I — Financial Requirements** | NTA and ASLF calculations, audited financial statements, cash flow projections, PI insurance details |
| **Part J — Disputes** | IDR procedures and EDR membership (AFCA Member #100204) |
| **Part K — Compensation Arrangements** | PI insurance details and certificate of currency |
| **Part L — Other Information** | Any additional matters ASIC should be aware of (e.g., related overseas licences, pending legal proceedings) |

**Practical Tips:**
- Draft all responses in a **separate document** before entering them into ASIC Connect — the portal can time out and does not auto-save frequently
- Keep responses **clear, concise, and specific** — avoid generic or boilerplate language
- Cross-reference ASIC's expectations in RG 2 for each section
- Have legal counsel review all responses before entry

**Responsible Manager Evidence Packs should include:**

- Certified copies of qualifications
- Detailed CV/resume with dates of employment

- Training completion certificates (RG 146 or equivalent)
- National Police Check (less than 12 months old)
- ASIC Banned and Disqualified Persons Register search result
- Personal Insolvency search (AFSA)

#### **Step 5: Conduct Internal Review**

Before lodgement, the complete application pack must be reviewed and signed off:

| Reviewer | Focus Area |
|---|---|
| **Legal Counsel** | Accuracy of authorisations sought; legal compliance of all documents; statutory declarations |
| **Compliance Officer** | Completeness against RG 2 checklist; consistency across all documents |
| **Finance** | Financial statements, NTA/ASLF calculations, PI insurance adequacy |
| **Directors** | Strategic alignment; business description accuracy; willingness to sign declarations |

**Internal review checklist:**
- [ ] All FS01 sections completed with no blank fields
- [ ] All supporting documents uploaded in PDF format
- [ ] RM evidence packs complete for each Responsible Manager
- [ ] Financial statements are audited and current (less than 12 months old)
- [ ] NTA meets minimum threshold for authorisations sought
- [ ] ASLF is positive
- [ ] PI insurance certificate of currency is valid and covers the relevant financial services
- [ ] AFCA membership confirmed and current
- [ ] AML/CTF Program provided (current version)
- [ ] TMDs prepared for each product
- [ ] All statutory declarations properly executed
- [ ] Directors have reviewed and approved the application
- [ ] Legal counsel has reviewed and confirmed no issues

---

#### **Step 6: Pay the Application Fee**

- The current ASIC application fee for an AFSL is **$4,189** (2025–26 schedule)
- Confirm the current fee at: https://asic.gov.au/about-asic/what-we-do/how-we-operate/asic-fees-and-charges/
- Payment is made online through ASIC Connect at the time of lodgement (credit card or direct debit)
- The fee is **non-refundable**, even if the application is refused or withdrawn
- Retain the payment receipt for records

---

#### **Step 7: Lodge the Application**

1. Log in to **ASIC Connect** at https://connectonline.asic.gov.au
2. Navigate to **AFS Licensing** > **Apply for a new AFS Licence**
3. Complete or confirm all sections of the FS01 form
4. Upload all supporting documents
5. Pay the application fee
6. Submit the application
7. **Record the lodgement confirmation number** — this is your primary reference for all future correspondence with ASIC
8. ASIC will send an acknowledgement email to the nominated contact within 5 business days

**Action Items post-lodgement:**
- [ ] Save lodgement confirmation (PDF and email)
- [ ] Record confirmation number in project tracking register
- [ ] Calendar reminders for ASIC's target assessment milestones
- [ ] Notify legal counsel and Directors of successful lodgement
- [ ] Set up a dedicated folder for ASIC correspondence

---

#### **Step 8: Manage the ASIC Assessment Process**

After lodgement, ASIC will assign a case officer. The assessment typically proceeds as follows:

| Stage | Typical Timeframe | Action Required |
|---|---|---|
| **Acknowledgement** | 5 business days | None — confirm receipt |
| **Completeness check** | 2–4 weeks | ASIC may return incomplete applications |
| **Substantive assessment** | 3–5 months | Case officer reviews all materials |
| **Request for Further Information (RFI)** | During assessment | Respond within the timeframe specified (typically 28 days) |
| **Second/subsequent RFI** | If required | Each RFI round may extend processing by 1–2 months |
| **Decision** | 150 business days target | Grant, grant with conditions, or refuse |

**Tips for managing RFIs:**
- Respond **promptly and comprehensively** — incomplete RFI responses generate further rounds
- Always have legal counsel review RFI responses before submission
- If you need more time, request an extension in writing before the deadline
- Treat every RFI as an opportunity to strengthen the application
- Keep a log of all ASIC communications with dates and response deadlines

### **5.0 PRE-LODGEMENT MEETING WITH ASIC**

ASIC offers **pre-lodgement meetings** for complex or novel applications. Given that cryptocurrency exchange AFSL applications are relatively new, CoinPort should **strongly consider requesting a pre-lodgement meeting**.

**How to request:**
- Email licensing@asic.gov.au with the subject line: "Pre-lodgement meeting request — AFS Licence — CoinPort Pty Ltd"
- Include a brief (1–2 page) summary of:
  - Company background and current regulatory registrations
  - Financial services and products to be authorised
  - Key questions or areas of uncertainty
- ASIC will typically schedule a meeting within 2–4 weeks

**Benefits:**
- Clarify which authorisations are appropriate for digital asset exchange services
- Understand ASIC's expectations and any areas of particular focus for crypto applicants
- Identify potential issues early, before formal lodgement
- Demonstrate good faith and regulatory engagement
- Potentially reduce the number of RFI rounds during assessment

#### 7.2 Key Observations from Industry Experience

Based on publicly available information, industry commentary, and ASIC publications:

1. **ASIC scrutiny of crypto applicants is elevated.** ASIC has publicly stated that digital asset platforms receive enhanced assessment due to the novel risk profile. Applications should anticipate additional questions on:
   - Custody arrangements and segregation of client assets
   - Technology risk (smart contract risk, blockchain risk, key management)
   - Market integrity controls (wash trading detection, manipulation prevention)
   - Adequacy of disclosure to retail clients
   - Valuation methodologies for digital assets

2. **Early movers had an advantage.** Independent Reserve and BTC Markets obtained AFSLs before the anticipated Digital Assets Framework legislation was introduced, when ASIC's approach to crypto was still evolving. Applicants under the new framework may face more prescriptive requirements.

3. **Processing times for crypto applications are longer.** Industry reports suggest that crypto-related AFSL applications have taken 9–18 months from lodgement to decision, compared to 5–9 months for traditional financial services applications.

4. **PI insurance remains a significant barrier.** Several exchanges have publicly noted the difficulty and cost of obtaining adequate PI insurance. The market for crypto PI insurance in Australia is thin, with only a handful of underwriters willing to quote.

5. **ASIC has issued mixed signals.** While encouraging innovation through the Innovation Hub, ASIC has also taken enforcement action against crypto businesses (e.g., the Finder Wallet enforcement action in 2022–23, the Block Earner Federal Court case in 2023–24). This demonstrates that ASIC expects full compliance with existing law regardless of whether specific crypto legislation has been enacted.

6. **The Digital Assets Framework Bill is a catalyst.** The introduction of the Corporations Amendment (Digital Assets Framework) Bill 2025 has increased urgency across the industry. Multiple exchanges are understood to be in various stages of AFSL preparation, even though the Bill has not yet received Royal Assent.

#### 7.3 Relevant ASIC Publications and Enforcement Actions

| Publication / Action | Date | Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **ASIC INFO 225 — Crypto-assets** | Updated 2024 | ASIC's guidance on when crypto-assets are financial products — essential reading for determining licence scope |
| **ASIC v Finder Wallet Pty Ltd** | 2022–23 | ASIC successfully argued that a crypto yield product was a financial product requiring an AFSL; reinforces the broad reach of financial product definitions |
| **ASIC v Web3 Ventures Pty Ltd (Block Earner)** | 2023–24 | Federal Court found that crypto-linked fixed yield products were financial products; ASIC secured declarations and penalties |
| **ASIC Report 705 — Crypto-assets: A Regulatory Update** | 2022 | ASIC's position on crypto regulation, licensing expectations, and enforcement priorities |
| **Treasury Consultation Paper — Regulating Digital Asset Platforms** | 2023–24 | Foundation for the Digital Assets Framework Bill; outlines the government's policy intent |
| **Senate Economics Legislation Committee Report** | Expected 2026 | Committee review of the Digital Assets Framework Bill — monitor for recommendations that may affect final legislation |

#### 7.4 Lessons Learned and Recommendations for CoinPort

Drawing on the industry experience above:

1. **Apply early, before the rush.** Once the Digital Assets Framework Bill receives Royal Assent, there will be a wave of AFSL applications from crypto businesses. Early applicants will benefit from shorter queues and more ASIC attention per application.

2. **Invest in the pre-lodgement meeting.** Exchanges that engaged with ASIC before lodging have reported smoother assessment processes. ASIC appreciates proactive engagement.

3. **Leverage existing compliance infrastructure.** CoinPort's AUSTRAC registration, AFCA membership, AML/CTF Program v3.1, and existing policy suite are significant advantages. Emphasise these in the application as evidence of a mature compliance culture.

4. **Over-prepare on custody and technology.** ASIC's primary concerns for crypto businesses centre on custody risk and technology resilience. Prepare detailed documentation on:
   - Hot/cold wallet architecture
   - Key management procedures
   - Disaster recovery and business continuity
   - Client asset segregation methodology
   - Third-party custodian due diligence (if applicable)

5. **Prepare for a long process.** Budget for 12–18 months from lodgement to decision. Ensure the business can sustain operations throughout the assessment period.

6. **Engage specialist legal counsel.** Generic corporate lawyers or even general financial services lawyers may lack the specific crypto-regulatory experience needed. Choose counsel who have successfully guided a crypto AFSL application to completion.

7. **Monitor peer activity.** Track announcements from other exchanges regarding their AFSL status. Industry bodies such as **Blockchain Australia** (https://blockchainaustralia.org) and the **Digital Law Association** (https://digitallawassociation.org) can provide updates and networking opportunities.

### **10.0 POST-LODGEMENT COMMUNICATION PROTOCOL**

Once lodged, maintain disciplined communication with ASIC:

| Principle | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Single point of contact** | Nominate the Compliance Officer as the primary ASIC contact; copy legal counsel on all correspondence |
| **Response timeframes** | Respond to all ASIC requests within the stipulated timeframe (typically 28 days); request extensions in writing if needed |
| **Record keeping** | Maintain a correspondence log with dates, ASIC reference numbers, and response deadlines |
| **Escalation** | Escalate any ASIC concern about authorisations, conditions, or potential refusal to Directors and legal counsel immediately |
| **Proactive updates** | If material changes occur during assessment (e.g., change of RM, change of business model, regulatory developments), notify ASIC promptly |
